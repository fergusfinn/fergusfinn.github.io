## Introduction

If you store a model's weights in bfloat16, each parameter gets 16 bits.
That's the budget. The question is whether we're spending it well.

Information theory gives us a clean way to ask this. Shannon entropy measures
the average information content per symbol in a stream of data. If every
possible byte value appears equally often, entropy is maximal and there's
nothing to squeeze out. If certain values dominate, entropy drops below the
bit-width, and the difference is waste: bits allocated but carrying no
information.

So that's the question, and it's the kind of thing we can just measure.

## Setup

We pulled weight files from as many open-weight models as we could get our
hands on [>1] — different labs (Qwen, DeepSeek, Google, OpenAI, Moonshot,
MiniMax, NVIDIA, StepFun, Zhipu), different scales (0.6B up to 1.4T
parameters), different storage formats (BF16, FP8, MXFP8, MXFP4, NVFP4,
INT4) — and computed the Shannon entropy of each.

[>1]: Coincidentally, we serve almost all of these models at [doubleword](https://app.doubleword.ai) - sign up today for 10$ in free tokens.

[Shannon's source coding theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shannon%27s_source_coding_theorem)
tells us that the minimum average number of bits needed to represent a single
symbol from an i.i.d. [>3] source is exactly its Shannon entropy. The gap
between the entropy of a model's weights and the bits a format actually
allocates is the slack we're looking for.

[>3]: Independently and identically distributed. Interestingly, for all intents
and purposes, LLM weights seem statistically to be i.i.d. I found this a little
bit surprising: you might imagine clusters of weights with similar roles
forming functional units within large matrices, and I assumed that the coupling
required during training to allow them to play these roles would couple their
distributions to one another. I mean we explicitly try to design these into the
architectures of MoE models! It seems like, to the extent that these functional
groups exist, they manage to become tailored to their role without these roles
manifesting in their distributions.

## The BF16 baseline

BF16 first. BF16 weights carry about 10.6 bits of
entropy per element, out of the 16 the format allocates. A third of the
budget is slack.

Why?

<EntropyBars client:load defaultFormats={["BF16"]} />

Across every BF16 model we measured, the slack is concentrated in one
place. The exponent carries about 2.6 bits of entropy, out of 8
allocated. The mantissa carries roughly 7 of its 7. The sign behaves like
a fair coin — 1 bit out of 1. Of the 16 bits we spend on a BF16 weight,
the only wasteful one is the exponent.

And the gap is remarkably stable. Two orders of magnitude in parameter
count, four labs, different training recipes, and the exponent entropy
lands in a band roughly 0.05 bits wide.


## Why the exponent?

The mantissa uses its full budget; the sign behaves like a fair coin. Only
the exponent has slack. So the question is what, about the way these
weights are distributed, makes the exponent specifically the wasteful
field.

A floating-point number's exponent is determined by its magnitude — roughly,
log₂|w|. So the entropy of the exponent across a tensor is a direct
function of how spread out the weight magnitudes are. If weights were
distributed evenly across all the magnitudes BF16 can represent, the
exponent would be near-maximally informative and we'd be using all 8 bits.
If they're all clustered in a narrow magnitude band, most of the 256
possible exponent values never appear, and the entropy collapses.

It turns out they're clustered. Every trained model we measured has its
weight magnitudes peaked sharply somewhere between $2^{-9}$ and $2^{-6}$ —
a [lognormal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Log-normal_distribution)-ish,
left-skewed, unimodal shape.

And it's not just that each model is narrow — they're all the *same*
narrow. Shift each distribution by its mean, rescale by its variance, and
(almost) every model collapses onto the same curve.

So the slack in BF16 isn't a property of any specific model or training
recipe. It's a property of the format combined with a universal fact about
how trained weights are distributed: BF16's exponent is sized for a wider
range of magnitudes than any model actually uses.

We're obviously not the first to notice that less precise number formats
are useful for LLMs — NVIDIA's headline FLOPS numbers have been doubling
on the back of halving bitwidths for a couple of years now. Do these
narrower formats cut out all the slack?

## FP8

The simplest narrower format is FP8. Same three-part structure as BF16,
allocations halved: 1/4/3 instead of 1/8/7.

The recipes that got these models to FP8 are all different — native FP8
pre-training, MXFP8 from QAT during post-training, BF16 plus a fine-grained
quantization pass, others — but the magnitude distribution at the byte
level is the same across all of them.[^q-origin]

FP8 weights carry about 6.5 bits of entropy out of 8, vs BF16's 10.6 of 16
— roughly 80% of the budget used, vs 66%. So shrinking the format does
close some slack, in absolute terms.

## Below the floor

Up to FP8, the magnitude distribution didn't have to move. Every byte-level
format we've looked at — BF16, FP8, MXFP8, Qwen's block-FP8 — gives the
per-element exponent at least 4 bits, comfortably more than the ~2.6 bits
of entropy the distribution wants. The slack is real but the format absorbs
it; the distribution sits inside whatever budget is available.

FP4 is where this breaks. The per-element exponent has 2 bits — 4 codes
for a distribution that wants something closer to 6. The per-element
field can't carry the magnitude distribution on its own.

The architectural workaround is the block scale. MXFP8 and MXFP4 use
32-element blocks with an E8M0 scale (just an 8-bit power-of-two exponent).
NVFP4 uses 16-element blocks with an E4M3 FP8 scale, plus a single FP32
per-tensor scale on top (so-called double-quantization). Qwen's block-FP8
uses 128-element blocks with BF16 scales. Moonshot's INT4 packs four-bit
integers with a higher-precision scale per group. The scale absorbs the
absolute magnitude per block; the per-element bits only have to cover
variation *within* the block.

Which raises the question: can the model arrange itself so within-block
ranges fit in 2 bits? If yes, the distribution has to adapt. If no,
information is lost.

<MagnitudeHistogram client:load defaultFormats={["MXFP4","NVFP4","INT4"]} />

It adapts. The magnitude histogram for sub-byte models doesn't sit on the
same lognormal curve that BF16 and FP8 do — it's narrower, often with a
different peak. The distribution that gets you low loss at MXFP4 isn't the
distribution that gets you low loss at BF16.

So below 8 bits, for the first time, the format is shaping the distribution
rather than the other way around. Up to FP8 the universality from §4 was a
property of the model: train under loose enough constraints and the weights
settle into the same lognormal shape. Below FP8, the constraint tightens
past the 2.6-bit floor, and the model has to give.

And we can see where the entropy went.

<EntropyBars client:load defaultFormats={["MXFP4","NVFP4","INT4"]} />

At MXFP4 the per-element exponent is fully used (~1.9 / 2) and the mantissa
is fully used (~1 / 1) — the slack has left the per-element bits. It hasn't
disappeared though: it's migrated into the per-block scales, which carry
about 0.03 bits of entropy per element out of 0.25 allocated for MXFP4.
NVFP4's larger scale field (FP8 rather than E8M0) closes more of that scale
slack — ~0.28 of 0.5.

Net per-element-plus-scale entropy: ~93% used at MXFP4, vs 80% at FP8 and
66% at BF16. Close to the floor, with most of the remaining slack now
living in the scales.

## Headroom

The remaining gap in each format is the headroom for entropy coding on
top: roughly a third of BF16's bits, ~17% of FP8's, ~7% of FP4's.

Whether it's worth recovering depends on where the bottleneck sits and
whether you can decode fast enough. We'll explore making use of that
headroom in followups. But it's real, consistent across the field, and
lossless — it's just bandwidth waiting to be traded for compute.
