Wide Expert Parallelism or 'wideEP' describes a common deployment pattern for
expert parallel models. The idea is roughly that we can go much wider with
expert parallelism than other parallelism strategies (tensor parallel), because
of EP's specific comms structure. This means we can continue with 'intra-model'
parallelism far beyond where it would be useful normally. Specifically, we can
go beyond a single 'scale-up' domain before we have to switch to Data
Parallelism over many replicas.

In this post, we go over some of the mathematics for when this paradigm
applies. We'll look at different model architectures, where wideEP benefits
over just increasing data parallelism, and how the ratio of scale up and scale
out bandwidth changes the game.

## Setup

Assume we have multiple nodes, each with a certain number of GPUs (usually 8,
but 4 on [isambard](previous post?)). The GPUs within each node are connected
with high bandwidth, all-to-all interconnect (note: topology is specific to
NVIDIA, but changing with other accelerators). We call the networking stack
inside a single node the 'scale-up' domain.

Each node is then connected to one another via 'scale-out' networking (this
ends at the datacenter, after which we end up in the public internet or private
backbones. We don't consider anything past scale-out). Scale out topology is
much more limited than scale-up topology. Specifically, we usually have 1 NIC
per GPU, NICs on each node are usually connected in 'rails', and the bandwidth
from 1 GPU to its corresponding GPU on another GPU is an order of magnitude
lower than the scale-up domain bandwidth (apart from anything else, it has to
be, since the NIC is addressed over PCIe. There have been rumours of NVIDIA
moving the NVLink C2C for the connections between their GPUs and NICs as they
move beyond PCIe v7 bandwidths though).

Let the intranode bandwidth be $BW_\mathrm{intra}$, and the internode be
$BW_\mathrm{inter}$.

Our goal is to do expert parallel inference. Assume a layer with $E$ experts
(to anchor, imagine $256$, as for DeepSeek-v4-flash (LINK) ). Per-token passed
into the MoE layer, $k$ of those experts are activated ($2$, for deepseek
flash). 

Let there be $W$ total accelerators, $M$ per GPU, $N$ nodes, $W=M \times N$.
A sequence has length $T_i$ (in
principle also some 'prompt' $S_i$ that's already been prefilled, but this is
not important for the FFNs). Each rank (or TP group) has individual batch size
$B_i$, and the global batch size $B=\sum_i B_i$.

In expert parallelism, the experts are split across all $W$ accelerators. The
rest of the model might be performed in 'data parallel' attention, in which
case each rank performs attention on a batch it owns specifically, with
attention weights replicated across each rank (common for MLA models). Or ranks
might be formed into 'tensor parallel' groups, of size at most equal to the
scale-up domain, where the weights are sharded across each accelerator in the
group, and each TP rank has a copy of the activations. The difference between
the two affects: 
1. the source ranks of the tokens.
2. to which ranks the results of the expert parallel layers must be communicated back to. 

At each layer, the activations (with hidden dimension $H$) must be communicated
to their experts, the expert matmul must be performed, and the result must be
communicated back to the dispatching rank. 

## How much communication do we have to do?

Per rank, we have $ M-1 $ scale up peers, $ W-M $ scale out peers. In a
rail-optimised fabric GPU $g$ on a node talks to GPU $g$ on every other node
through rail switch $g$; to reach a GPU $g' \neq g$ off-node you take one extra
intra-node hop, either out on your own rail and across NVLink on the far side,
or across NVLink locally and then out on rail $g'$. 

The volume each rank dispatches is set by its own batch, not by how wide we
go. A rank pushes its $B_i$ tokens, $k$ experts apiece, and with experts spread
evenly across $W$ accelerators a fraction $M/W = 1/N$ of those destinations sit
on the local node while $1 - 1/N$ are off it. Counting both the dispatch and the
combine, the bytes a rank moves over scale-out per layer is:

$$
V_\mathrm{inter} \;\approx\; 2\,B_i\,k\,H\,b_a\,\Big(1-\tfrac{1}{N}\Big),
\qquad
t_\mathrm{inter} \;\approx\; \frac{2\,B_i\,k\,H\,b_a\,(1-1/N)}{BW_\mathrm{inter}},
$$

where $b_a$ is the bytes per activation element and the factor of two is the
round trip. The argument for wideEP is basically $(1 - 1/N)$ term. 

The
per-rank volume depends on the rank's own $B_i k$ and nothing else; widening EP
does not add traffic, it only shifts the mix from intra-node to inter-node, and
that mix saturates almost immediately ($1 - 1/N \to 1$ after a couple of nodes).
This is exactly what tensor parallelism cannot do — there the per-rank
all-reduce volume grows with the group size, which is why TP is pinned inside a
single scale-up domain while EP can run across many.


The tradeoff that you're trying to win when you do wideEP is: you free up HBM
by sharding your experts, which means you can keep more sequences in flight.
For each sequence, you have to pay the scale out comms, but the matmuls once
you've sent the activations (for the new tokens) can be near free.

Each rank stores $E/W$ experts rather than all $E$, freeing
roughly $E\,w_E\,(1 - 1/W)$ bytes of weight per layer (with $w_E$ the bytes in
one expert's weights). That memory becomes KV cache, which becomes a larger
$B_i$, which becomes throughput — provided we were capacity-bound on batch in
the first place. WideEP is a way of spending scale-out bandwidth to buy back
HBM.

What we spend is the all-to-all latency, and whether that costs us anything
depends on whether it hides behind the expert GEMM[>2]. While it does, a given
width is free: we keep banking KV cache at no exposed latency. The condition is
a race between the per-rank weight load and the exposed comms,

$$
\underbrace{\frac{(E/W)\,w_E}{BW_\mathrm{HBM}}}_{\text{weight load},\ \sim 1/W}
\;\gtrsim\;
\underbrace{\frac{2\,B_i\,k\,H\,b_a\,(1-1/N)}{BW_\mathrm{inter}}}_{\text{exposed all-to-all}},
$$

with $BW_\mathrm{HBM}$ the per-accelerator HBM bandwidth. The left side shrinks
as we widen; the right side is flat. They cross at

$$
W_\mathrm{max} \;\approx\;
\frac{E\,w_E}{BW_\mathrm{HBM}} \cdot
\frac{BW_\mathrm{inter}}{2\,B_i\,k\,H\,b_a\,(1-1/N)}.
$$

Below $W_\mathrm{max}$ we widen: the all-to-all hides behind the GEMM and every
extra rank frees more memory. Above it the step has gone comms-bound, more width
buys nothing, and the right move is to stop widening and add data-parallel
replicas instead. The ratio $BW_\mathrm{inter}/BW_\mathrm{HBM}$ sitting in
$W_\mathrm{max}$ is the knob the hardware hands us: as scale-out bandwidth climbs
toward HBM bandwidth, the useful EP width climbs with it.

[>2]: This is the overlap that dual-batch / two-batch-overlap schemes are
chasing — run one microbatch's expert compute while the other's dispatch is in
flight, so the comms term in the inequality is genuinely hidden rather than
merely small.
