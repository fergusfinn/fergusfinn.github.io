Wide Expert Parallelism or 'wideEP' describes a common deployment pattern for
expert parallel models. The idea is roughly that we can go much wider with
expert parallelism than other parallelism strategies (tensor parallel), because
of EP's specific comms structure. This means we can continue with 'intra-model'
parallelism far beyond where it would be useful normally. Specifically, we can
go beyond a single 'scale-up' domain before we have to switch to Data
Parallelism over many replicas.

In this post, we go over some of the mathematics for when this paradigm
applies. We'll look at different model architectures, where wideEP benefits
over just increasing data parallelism, and how the ratio of scale up and scale
out bandwidth changes the game.

## Setup

Assume we have multiple nodes, each with a certain number of GPUs (usually 8,
but 4 on [isambard](previous post?)). The GPUs within each node are connected
with high bandwidth, all-to-all interconnect (note: topology is specific to
NVIDIA, but changing with other accelerators). We call the networking stack
inside a single node the 'scale-up' domain.

Each node is then connected to one another via 'scale-out' networking (this
ends at the datacenter, after which we end up in the public internet or private
backbones. We don't consider anything past scale-out). Scale out topology is
much more limited than scale-up topology. Specifically, we usually have 1 NIC
per GPU, NICs on each node are usually connected in 'rails', and the bandwidth
from 1 GPU to its corresponding GPU on another GPU is an order of magnitude
lower than the scale-up domain bandwidth (apart from anything else, it has to
be, since the NIC is addressed over PCIe. There have been rumours of NVIDIA
moving the NVLink C2C for the connections between their GPUs and NICs as they
move beyond PCIe v7 bandwidths though).

Let the intranode bandwidth be $BW_\mathrm{intra}$, and the internode be
$BW_\mathrm{inter}$.

Our goal is to do expert parallel inference. Assume a layer with $E$ experts
(to anchor, imagine $256$, as for DeepSeek-v4-flash (LINK) ). Per-token passed
into the MoE layer, $k$ of those experts are activated ($2$, for deepseek
flash).

Let there be $W$ total accelerators, $M$ per GPU, $N$ nodes, $W=M \times N$.
A sequence has length $T_i$ (in
principle also some 'prompt' $S_i$ that's already been prefilled, but this is
not important for the FFNs). Each rank (or TP group) has individual batch size
$B_i$, and the global batch size $B=\sum_i B_i$.

In expert parallelism, the experts are split across all $W$ accelerators. The
rest of the model might be performed in 'data parallel' attention, in which
case each rank performs attention on a batch it owns specifically, with
attention weights replicated across each rank (common for MLA models). Or ranks
might be formed into 'tensor parallel' groups, of size at most equal to the
scale-up domain, where the weights are sharded across each accelerator in the
group, and each TP rank has a copy of the activations. The difference between
the two affects:

1. the source ranks of the tokens.
2. to which ranks the results of the expert parallel layers must be communicated back to.

At each layer, the activations (with hidden dimension $H$) must be communicated
to their experts, the expert matmul must be performed, and the result must be
communicated back to the dispatching rank.

## How much communication do we have to do?

Per rank, we have $ M-1 $ scale up peers, $ W-M $ scale out peers. In a
rail-optimised fabric GPU $g$ on a node talks to GPU $g$ on every other node
through rail switch $g$; to reach a GPU $g' \neq g$ off-node you take one extra
intra-node hop, either out on your own rail and across NVLink on the far side,
or across NVLink locally and then out on rail $g'$.

When deciding how to scale up a deployment, we always have the simple option:
just do more copies, with load balancing between them (data parallelism). This
gives us linear scaling of throughput per GPU.

The quesion we ought to ask ourselves: can we do better? Superlinear implies
that our method of scaling is doing something other than the same thing $N$
times. For example, the TTFT of a fixed single prefill scales superlinearly
with tensor parallelism, since more GPUs are brought to bear on the same
sequence.

The way to get throughput to scale superlinearly is to increase our per GPU
batch size. We have massive headroom to do this without becoming compute bound,
since MoEs are so memory hungry. To increase our per-GPU batch size, we need to
increase the per GPU KV cache size, since this is usually what binds. To
increase the KV cache size, we need to share weights between different
accelerators.

I think this is the right way to think about EP -- as a _weight-replication
continuum_ -- with DP (all experts present on all GPUs) on one end, and EP
(each expert present on only one GPU) on the other. At the DP end, we do no
communications; at the EP end, we do a lot of communications. The choice that
takes us along the continuum: is it cheaper to keep our experts somewhere else,
and send our tokens to them experts, than it is to keep them here, but pay a
tax in KV cache space.

From the perspective of a single $M$-GPU node. Our experts take up $K$GB of KV
cache space per GPU. Each GPU has some fixed amount of HBM it can use to store
the weights. Assume DPA - so we have to replicate the full attention weights on
each rank.

The choice to go expert parallel goes through three phases:

1. First: is it possible to fit the whole model on a single node? If its not,
   then we're going to have to offload some experts to other nodes to get
   serving at all, and we're going to start off comms bound by inter node
   bandwidth.
2. Second: what's the ratio of our cross-node bandwidth to our internode
   bandwidth? The optimal number of experts to offload, so we are equally bound
   by intra and inter-node bandwidth, is given by the ratio of the inter to the
   intra node bandwidth.
3. Third: Is it valuable to become comms bound by the cross-node bandwidth in
   exchange for even more KV cache space?

The answers depend on lots of different parameters: the model, the bandwidths,
the GPUs HBM sizes, the sequence lengths, the phase (prefill/decode/spec. dec.)
Some of these parameters change over time (the sequence length, the phase).

We haven't even mentioned overlap. There are a few different approaches, mostly
classifiable as inter-kernel or intra-kernel overlap. DeepEP & TBO are the
inter-kernel idea: you split each batch into two microbatches, and then do the
comms of batch A while youre doing the compute of batch B etc. This halves your
batch size, so halves the weight amortization in the expert compute. Or you can
do intrakernel (mKernel, comet, megaMoE, etc.), where the comms and compute
happen withing the same shared MoE megakernel. The problem then is you have
to write the whole thing, and can't seperately rely on optimized
implementations of EP & GEMM kernels.

## Appendix: the decode-throughput model

The explorer plots one number — per-GPU decode throughput, tokens/s — as a
memory-bandwidth roofline. Everything below is what it computes; it's
deliberately a _ceiling_ (HBM traffic and all-to-all only, no kernel overhead),
so treat the absolute heights as upper bounds and the _shapes_ as the result.

**Setup.** Let $w = E/E_g$ be the effective EP width — the number of GPUs one
full expert copy is spread across, $E_g$ experts held per GPU. The weight splits
in two: a replicated part $W_\mathrm{dense}$ (attention, embeddings, shared
experts, dense FFN, router, and — for hybrids — the Mamba mixers), present on
every rank, and the routed experts $W_\mathrm{exp}$, sharded by $w$. So per-GPU
weight is

$$
W(w) = W_\mathrm{dense} + \frac{W_\mathrm{exp}}{w}.
$$

Both are counted in _bytes at their real serving dtype_ — e.g. DeepSeek-V4's
experts are FP4 (0.5 B) while its attention is FP8, so $W_\mathrm{dense}$ is a
mixed-precision sum, not a param count times one dtype.

**Batch.** Whatever HBM the weights leave goes to KV cache, and each sequence
costs $T\kappa$ bytes of KV ($\kappa$ = KV bytes/token at the serving cache
dtype) plus, for a hybrid, a fixed recurrent state $S$:

$$
B(w) = \frac{C - W(w)}{T\kappa + S}.
$$

$C$ is HBM/GPU. Widening $w$ shrinks the weight term and grows $B$ — the entire
point of wideEP.

**The decode step.** Each step is memory-bound, so its time is the HBM traffic
over bandwidth, plus the exposed all-to-all:

$$
t_\mathrm{step} = \underbrace{\frac{W(w) + \overbrace{B\,T\kappa}^{\text{KV read}} + \overbrace{2BS}^{\text{state I/O}}}{BW_\mathrm{HBM}}}_{t_\mathrm{HBM}} + t_\mathrm{comms}.
$$

Three HBM terms: stream the weights once; attention re-reads the _entire_ KV
cache every step ($B\,T\kappa$); and a Mamba layer reads and writes its recurrent
state ($2BS$, zero for pure transformers). This KV-read term is what was missing
from a naïve "throughput = batch / weight-read" model, and it's what makes
throughput saturate rather than grow without bound[>1].

[>1]: Without it, $\Phi = B/(W/BW_\mathrm{HBM})$ grows linearly with batch until
comms; with it, $\Phi$ rises to the $BW_\mathrm{HBM}/(T\kappa)$ plateau. The KV
read is usually the dominant decode term at long context, so omitting it isn't a
small error.

**Communication.** Per layer a token sends its activation to $k$ experts and gets
a result back — $2\,k\,H_\mathrm{disp}\,b_a$ bytes over $L_\mathrm{moe}$ MoE layers
($H_\mathrm{disp}$ is the dispatched width: the hidden size, or a smaller latent
for a model that down-projects before dispatch; $b_a$ is bytes per dispatched
element). Under fill-the-node-first placement a needed expert sits on the same
GPU with probability $f_\mathrm{local} = \min(1, 1/w)$, elsewhere on the node up
to $f_\mathrm{on} = \min(1, M/w)$, and off-node with
$f_\mathrm{off} = \max(0, 1 - M/w)$. The on-node and off-node legs run
concurrently, so we take the slower:

$$
t_\mathrm{comms} = B \cdot 2 L_\mathrm{moe} k H_\mathrm{disp} b_a \cdot \max\!\left(\frac{f_\mathrm{on} - f_\mathrm{local}}{BW_\mathrm{intra}},\ \frac{f_\mathrm{off}}{BW_\mathrm{inter}}\right).
$$

**Throughput, and the ceiling.** Per-GPU decode rate is
$\Phi(w) = B(w)/t_\mathrm{step}$. As $w \to \infty$ the weight term vanishes and
the KV read fills HBM, so

$$
\Phi \to \frac{BW_\mathrm{HBM}}{T\kappa},
$$

a hard per-GPU ceiling set by KV bandwidth alone — independent of batch, weights,
and width. Big-$\kappa$ models (MiniMax's 4-KV-head GQA, GLM's MLA at 78 layers)
hit a low ceiling; small-$\kappa$ models (DeepSeek MLA in FP8) a high one.

**Bandwidths.** Measured on the Isambard 4×GH200 node, per direction (a dispatch
is directional): $BW_\mathrm{intra} = 450$ GB/s (NVLink-4, 18 links ×
25 GB/s/dir — the aggregate a balanced all-to-all reaches; the quad is
switchless, so a _single_ peer gets only 6 links ≈ 150 GB/s, but that doesn't
bind a balanced all-to-all) and $BW_\mathrm{inter} = 25$ GB/s (one 200 Gb/s
Slingshot NIC per GPU).

**What's idealised** — uniformly, so the _shape_ survives even where the absolute
number is optimistic: memory-bound compute (the expert GEMM stays HBM-bound below
~256 tokens/expert, true in this regime; attention and Mamba decode are
memory-bound by construction); data-parallel attention; perfect overlap of the
two comms legs but _no_ compute/comms overlap (so $t_\mathrm{HBM}$ and
$t_\mathrm{comms}$ add); and mean-field routing — balanced load with no expert
skew, the one place the switchless quad's per-peer limit would actually bite.
