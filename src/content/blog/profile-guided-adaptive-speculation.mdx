The companion post, [the economics of speculative
decoding](https://fergusfinn.com/blog/economics-of-speculative-decoding/),
works out what a speculated token costs to verify on a modern MoE model, and
finds that the answer moves: with batch size, with context length, with where
the step sits on the roofline. A token that is free to verify at one operating
point costs nearly full price at another. That post ended with a static
conclusion, a chart you could read $\gamma^\star$ off, given your operating
point.

This post takes the next step, and makes the deployment decide for itself. Two
mechanisms, in increasing order of ambition:

1. **Price the draft length per scheduler step.** The engine knows its decode
   batch the instant before the drafter runs. Pricing every candidate draft
   length through the cost model and keeping the best one replaces the offline
   $\gamma$ sweep entirely.

2. **Gate each sequence on the drafter's own confidence.** The drafter
   produces a probability for every token it proposes, and that probability
   turns out to be a well-calibrated predictor of whether the verifier will
   keep the token. Sequences whose drafts are going to die shouldn't pay for
   deep verification; sequences in easy territory should run long. This is a
   per-sequence, per-step decision, and the signal is free.

The headline, measured end to end: a single adaptive policy matches the best
statically-tuned draft length at every steady operating point with no tuning
at all, beats it by 10--23% at the operating points where verification is
expensive, and under realistic time-varying load avoids failure modes that no
static choice can: the best fixed $\gamma$ at one burst intensity is the wrong
one at another, and the deepest setting quietly starves prefill.

Everything below is one model on one GPU: Qwen3.6-35B-A3B (a 35B-total,
3B-active hybrid: GatedDeltaNet linear-attention layers interleaved 3:1 with
full GQA attention, MoE on every layer, and a native single-layer MTP head
that serves as the drafter) on a B200.

## The signal: drafters know when they're guessing

Speculative decoding already measures everything it needs. Each verification
step tells you exactly how many drafted tokens survived, and the drafter
produces a probability for each token it proposes. The question is whether
that probability is informative enough to act on.

We collected per-token traces of MTP speculation: Qwen3.6-35B-A3B with its
own MTP head as drafter, running real generation over a benchmark spanning
eleven domains and context lengths from 1k to 32k, logging for every drafted
token its draft-time probability and whether the verifier committed it. Two
datasets: 46k draft rounds at depth 4, and 12k rounds at depth 8 (185k and
97k drafted tokens respectively).

The drafter's confidence predicts acceptance remarkably well[>1]:

| draft-prob decile | mean draft prob | P(commit) |
|---:|---:|---:|
| bottom | 0.23 | 0.135 |
| 3rd | 0.57 | 0.39 |
| 5th | 0.86 | 0.62 |
| 7th | 0.99 | 0.86 |
| top | 1.00 | 0.935 |

[>1]: AUC 0.91 at the first draft position, 0.81--0.84 at deeper positions.
Entropy of the draft distribution carries essentially the same signal
(AUC within 0.01), so the chosen-token logprob, which is the cheapest thing
to extract, is also sufficient.

A 7× spread in survival probability, visible at draft time, before any
verification has been paid for. The per-depth acceptance figures usually
quoted (here: 0.84 at depth one falling to 0.21 by depth eight,
unconditionally) hide this: conditioned on having survived so far, acceptance
is nearly flat at ~0.8 per position. What actually varies is *which sequences*
are in easy territory, and the drafter knows.

## What gating is worth, before touching an engine

The traces let us answer the value question exactly, with no simulation and no
engine work. Drafting in the traces was unconditional at fixed depth, and
committed tokens form a monotone prefix of each round, so any *truncation*
policy can be evaluated off-policy: truncate a round to length $G$ and it
commits exactly $\min(\text{actual prefix}, G) + 1$ tokens. We fit the
confidence-to-acceptance calibration on half the rounds and evaluate policies
on the other half.

The objective is committed tokens per unit verify cost, with the cost of a
round $1 + r \cdot G$: one mandatory token plus $G$ draft slots at marginal
price $r$. The price $r \in [0, 1]$ is where the companion post's roofline
analysis enters, summarized in a single number: $r \to 0$ deep in the
memory-bound region (verification rides along free), $r \to 1$ at the compute
bound (every verify slot pays full freight).

Three policies. The **best fixed $G$** at each $r$, which is what an offline
sweep gives you, and also what trailing-average acceptance adaptation
converges to in steady state[>2]. The **gated** policy: extend each round's
draft while the calibrated survival estimate of the next slot beats the
marginal price. And the **oracle**: verify exactly the tokens that will
commit, an upper bound no causal policy reaches.

[>2]: This matters for situating the contribution: dynamic draft length per
se is shipped technology -- sglang's `--speculative-adaptive` adjusts a global
depth from an acceptance-length EMA with hysteresis. In steady state that
recovers the best fixed depth for the current workload. What it cannot do is
distinguish sequences within a batch, or react to the *cost* side (load)
rather than the acceptance side.

Committed tokens per unit cost on the held-out depth-8 rounds:

| $r$ | best fixed $G$ | fixed | gated | oracle | gated vs fixed |
|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| 0.05 | 8 | 3.26 | 3.48 | 3.88 | +6.6% |
| 0.10 | 6 | 2.56 | 2.91 | 3.37 | +13.6% |
| 0.20 | 4 | 1.92 | 2.26 | 2.67 | +17.8% |
| 0.30 | 3 | 1.59 | 1.89 | 2.21 | **+19.0%** |
| 0.50 | 2 | 1.25 | 1.47 | 1.64 | +17.8% |
| 0.70 | 1 | 1.08 | 1.22 | 1.31 | +12.5% |
| 1.00 | 0 | 1.00 | 1.00 | 1.00 | 0.0% |

Across the middle of the price range, where the companion post's analysis says
real deployments of this class of model actually sit, per-sequence gating is
worth 12--19% over the best fixed draft length, and captures half to
two-thirds of the oracle headroom. At the extremes the gain vanishes, exactly
as it should: when verification is free you speculate maximally for everyone,
when it costs full price you don't speculate at all, and in neither case is
there a decision left to inform. The gate also *dominates* rather than
trading off: at $r = 0.3$ it commits more tokens per round than the best
fixed choice (3.75 vs 3.01) at barely more verify width (4.29 vs 4.0),
because it spends its width on rounds that survive.

## The two policies

Both run at the same point in the engine loop: the end of a scheduler step,
when the decode batch for the next step is known and the drafter is about to
run.

**Budget** (per-step, batch-level). For every candidate depth $G$ from 0 to
the cap, price a verify of width $1+G$ over the actual decode batch through
the cost model, and keep

$$
G^\star = \arg\max_G \; \frac{\mathbb{E}[\text{accepted} \mid G] + 1}{C(B, 1 + G, S)}.
$$

The acceptance expectation comes from the measured curve; the cost from the
roofline model (in the simulator) or a profiled step-time table (on the real
engine). No sweep, no foresight: the inputs are the batch in hand and a
running acceptance measurement.

**Gated** (per-step, per-sequence). Compute each sequence's survival chain --
the running product of calibrated acceptance estimates from its drafted
tokens' logprobs -- then allocate draft slots greedily, highest survival
first, while the marginal slot improves expected committed tokens per unit
step time. The marginal cost of a slot comes from the same cost curve the
budget policy prices; the drafter is charged on the deepest draft in the
batch. A sequence with a confident chain keeps its full depth; a sequence
whose first draft already looks dead gets a plain decode step. The budget
policy is the special case where every sequence has the same signal.

## Does it hold up in a full engine loop? (simulation)

Off-policy analysis prices rounds independently with a scalar $r$. A real
engine couples everything: the roofline prices the whole step, sequences
share weight and cache transfers, schedulers trim drafts when the token
budget runs out, prefill competes with verification. Before touching a GPU we
ran both policies through a discrete-event simulator of a vLLM-style engine
([inference-lab](https://github.com/doublewordai/inference-lab)) that models
all of this: the MoE coupon-collector expert loading, the hybrid
GDN+GQA attention costs, chunked prefill, KV limits, and -- crucially --
trace-driven acceptance: each simulated decode step draws a *real* round from
the held-out traces, so the gate sees the true joint distribution of its
signal and the outcome, including its own calibration error.

### Steady load

Closed-loop concurrency sweep, ~1.1k-token inputs / ~0.9k outputs, output
tokens per second:

| conc | no-spec | best fixed $\gamma$ | budget | gated | gated vs best fixed |
|---:|---:|:---|---:|---:|---:|
| 1 | 1,369 | 2,064 (γ3) | 2,064 | 2,496 | **+20.9%** |
| 4 | 2,697 | 3,198 (γ2) | 3,198 | 3,919 | **+22.6%** |
| 16 | 4,117 | 8,309 (γ8) | 8,312 | 8,310 | +0.0% |
| 64 | 7,724 | 29,458 (γ8) | 29,497 | 29,229 | −0.8% |
| 256 | 19,210 | 63,698 (γ8) | 64,404 | 65,611 | +3.0% |
| 1,024 | 29,487 | 68,415 (γ6) | 71,567 | 75,674 | **+10.6%** |
| 4,096 | 30,007 | 68,558 (γ6) | 72,005 | 75,566 | **+10.2%** |

Three regimes, exactly where the cost model puts them. At concurrency 1--4
the batch sits on the MoE expert-tax shoulder: every verify token drags in
fresh experts, verification is expensive, and per-sequence gating is worth
+21--23%. In the memory-bound middle verification is nearly free, every
policy correctly speculates to the cap, and there is nothing to win -- though
the gate gets there with ~30% less verify work (at concurrency 16 it chooses
mean depth 5.7 and matches fixed-8 to four digits)[>3]. Past the compute
bound the marginal price rises again and the gate is worth +10%.

[>3]: The −0.8% at concurrency 64 is the cost of estimating the marginal
price by interpolating the homogeneous cost curve at the mean verify width;
convexity makes that a slight overestimate when most sequences run deep and a
few are truncated, so the gate trims a handful of slots that were actually
free. Half a percent seems a fair price for a policy that needs no
per-sequence cost evaluation.

The budget column is the no-tuning claim: at every operating point it sits on
the best fixed value, found by reading the batch, not by sweeping. Its gains
over best-fixed at 1,024+ come from mixing depths across steps as the decode
batch composition shifts, something a single $\gamma$ cannot do.

### Time-varying load

Steady-state sweeps understate the case for adaptivity, because a deployment
doesn't get to pick its operating point. We drove the simulated engine with a
square-wave load: 300-second bursts at rate $\lambda$ alternating with
300-second troughs at 2 requests/s (decode batch of 1--4, the expensive
shoulder), and swept the burst intensity. Burst-window e2e p99 (seconds) and
trough-window mean TPOT (milliseconds):

| burst $\lambda$ (req/s) | best fixed | budget | gated |
|---:|:---|:---|:---|
| 60 | γ8: 9.3 s / 1.04 ms | 9.3 / 0.90 | 9.4 / **0.65** |
| 130 | γ4: 20.8 s / 0.98 ms | 26.9 / 0.94 | 22.7 / **0.68** |
| 200 | γ2: 108 s / 0.86 ms | 139 / 0.85 | **107** / **0.61** |

Read the "best fixed" column first: it's a different $\gamma$ at every burst
intensity (8, then 4, then 2). The operator who tuned at one traffic level
holds the wrong setting at the next. The gated policy tracks each point's
envelope within 9% without being told the traffic level, beats every fixed
setting outright once the bursts genuinely overload the engine, and wins
every trough by ~30% on per-token latency -- the regime where an interactive
user is actually watching tokens arrive.

One failure mode deserves its own sentence. Fixed $\gamma = 8$ -- the setting
a closed-loop throughput benchmark at high concurrency would recommend --
posts a time-to-first-token p99 of *14 seconds* during moderate bursts
(adaptive: 0.12 s), and 181 seconds at high intensity. Deep static drafts eat
the token budget that chunked prefill needs, and arriving requests queue
behind speculation that mostly gets rejected. None of the adaptive policies
exhibit this, not because they were designed to protect prefill, but because
the verify width they buy is priced, and at those operating points deep
drafts don't pay.

The chosen depth, watched over a burst cycle, is the whole mechanism in one
trace: mean draft length ~7 during the burst (batch ~600, memory-bound,
verification cheap), dropping to ~3 in the trough (batch ~2, expert tax,
verification dear) -- with the per-sequence spread inside each step doing the
fine adjustment.

## On real hardware

The simulator's cost model is a roofline, and rooflines are polite fictions.
The real test is the same policies inside a production engine, with all the
machinery the simulator abstracts away: CUDA graphs, chunked prefill,
streaming, scheduler overheads, and an MoE dispatch whose cost structure is
whatever it is rather than whatever we modelled.

**Implementation.** All four policies run as a ~300-line plugin on stock vLLM
v1 (no fork, no rebuild), loaded through the engine's plugin mechanism so it
reaches the process that owns the model. vLLM v1 turns out to be
structurally ready for per-sequence draft lengths: the rejection sampler
already takes a per-request draft count, and the scheduler already truncates
each request's proposed draft list. The plugin intercepts exactly one seam --
the moment the proposed drafts leave the model runner for the scheduler --
and shortens each request's list according to the policy. The drafter's
chain logprobs are captured during drafting (one extra log-softmax per draft
step in this prototype; a production version would fuse the gather into the
draft sampler). Fixed-$\gamma$, acceptance-EMA, budget, and gated are then
the same code path with different keep-length functions, which is the
fairness property the comparison needs[>4].

[>4]: Two honesty notes. The fixed-$k$ arms truncate an 8-deep drafter
rather than configuring a $k$-deep one, so they pay the full drafter cost a
native deployment wouldn't; the vanilla arm (no truncation) and the no-spec
arm are native. And the gated arm carries its logprob-capture overhead in
every number reported.

**The cost model is a table, not a formula.** Instead of porting the
roofline, the cost input to the budget and gated policies is profiled on the
target itself: a calibration pass sweeps decode batch size and verify width
and records the median wall-clock gap between engine steps -- not the model
forward time, the whole step. This distinction earned its keep twice. In
eager mode, forward-only timings said a verify slot was nearly free while
realized step costs said width 9 cost twice width 3 (the difference living
in drafter catch-up, rejection sampling, and host dispatch between
forwards). And under CUDA graphs, truncating a draft turns out to carry a
real cost of its own: the engine captures full graphs for the vanilla verify
shape, and a truncated batch falls off the fast path. Because the
calibration runs through the same truncation machinery as the policies, that
implementation tax lands *inside* the measured $C(B, g)$, and the policies
only truncate where it pays net of the engine's actual warts. A modelled
cost would have confidently recommended truncations that lose money; the
profiled one cannot, by construction.

### What the engine taught us before any numbers

Three facts about adaptive speculation on a production engine, none of which
the simulator -- or any roofline -- predicts, all of which we measured the
hard way:

1. **There is a cliff, and it moves.** vLLM captures full CUDA graphs for
   batches up to a token budget set by *free GPU memory at boot*. A verify
   batch that crosses it falls off the fast path and step time roughly
   triples. On one container, vanilla $\gamma{=}8$ at concurrency 64 (576
   verify tokens) cleared the budget and won; on another boot of the *same
   image on the same GPU type*, it fell over the edge and ran *slower than
   no speculation at all*. The optimal fixed draft length is not even a
   property of (model, GPU, workload) -- it depends on how much graph memory
   that particular boot got.

2. **The cost of a width is a property of the width sequence.** Steady
   width-9 decode steps cost 10.9 ms at batch 1 on our reference container;
   an isolated width-9 step sandwiched between width-8 steps costs ~18 ms.
   Changing shape pays a transition penalty. This breaks naive measurement
   (a single-step exploration probe mis-prices the width it probes) and
   naive policies (a per-sequence gate at batch 1 changes shape nearly
   every step and bleeds the penalty continuously). Our cost learner only
   accepts samples from runs of three or more same-shape steps, and the
   budget policy explores in held blocks and switches widths with
   hysteresis -- not for stability, for *cost*.

3. **Truncation itself is taxed, asymmetrically.** Keeping all 8 drafts is
   a no-op that leaves the engine's fast path untouched; keeping 7 is a new
   shape. So $C(B, 8) < C(B, 7)$ -- cost is genuinely non-monotone in draft
   length, and an early version of our learner that assumed monotonicity
   poisoned its own fast-path estimate with taxed samples and walked away
   from the optimum.

Each of these went into the policy as a constraint the profiled cost table
either encodes (the cliff, the truncation tax) or that the learning
machinery respects (steady-state-only samples, block exploration, sticky
switching). None of them could have been written down from first principles
before touching the engine, which is the strongest form of this post's
argument: the cost side of speculation has to be *measured on the
deployment*, continuously.

The shape of the result, established across five independent containers:

the online learner finds the truncation that static configuration cannot:
on a cliff-exposed container, budget beat vanilla by **+55% at concurrency
64** and +21% at 256, in the same run that cost it 8--17% at the
concurrencies where exploration bought nothing. The acceptance-EMA
baseline, which is healthy acceptance-side adaptation, loses ~30--40% at
small batch on every container we ran: acceptance tells it to sit at tier 3
while the cost structure makes deep drafts free. Cost-blindness is not a
strawman; it is the shipped state of the art.

### Per-sequence gating, honestly

The trace analysis says per-sequence gating is worth 12--19% over any
single depth; the batch-coupled simulator agrees (+10--23% at the expensive
ends of the roofline). On the real engine, the prototype captures this only
where CUDA-graph bucket padding absorbs per-sequence width variation (+9%
over vanilla at concurrency 256 on a non-cliff container) and loses
elsewhere to the shape-transition tax that per-sequence freedom maximally
provokes. With the capture overhead measured at a uniform 4--5% (ablation:
vanilla with and without logprob extraction), the accounting is clear: the
*signal* is real and free, the *allocation mechanism* is what today's
engine taxes. The feature this motivates is first-class ragged verification
-- per-sequence draft lengths as a supported shape, not a fallback -- and
until then the production policy is the homogeneous budget with the gate's
machinery held in reserve.

## Appendix: trace semantics and the off-policy evaluation

The traces log, for every drafted token, the tuple (config, domain, depth,
draft logprob, draft entropy, committed). The committed bit comes from the
verifier's output: in vLLM's rejection sampler the sampled-token tensor
carries a placeholder after the first rejection, so "committed" at depth $d$
is the *unconditional* event that the round's surviving prefix reached depth
$d$. Per-round commits are therefore a monotone prefix (we verified this
holds for 100% of reconstructed rounds), conditional per-position acceptance
is the ratio of adjacent unconditional rates (~0.8, nearly flat in depth),
and truncation policies can be evaluated exactly: a round truncated to $G$
commits $\min(\text{prefix}, G) + 1$.

Rounds are reconstructed from row order (drafting was unconditional at fixed
depth, so rows cycle through depths within each generation call). The
calibration -- 20 quantile bins of draft probability for pooled conditional
acceptance, with a per-depth multiplicative correction -- is fit on a random
half of rounds; every number quoted is measured on the other half.

## Appendix: the acceptance-EMA baseline

The strongest shipped point of comparison is sglang's `--speculative-adaptive`:
a global draft depth driven by an exponential moving average of accepted
length, with hysteresis over a tier list. We ported it faithfully into the
same engine and hook point as our policies (EMA $\alpha=0.2$, one update
every 5 verifies after a 10-verify warmup, tiers $\{1, 3, 7, \gamma_{\max}\}$,
down-hysteresis $-0.25$): the depth drops to the tier below when the EMA
falls more than 0.75 below it, rises when it clears the current tier minus
0.5. Two structural limits follow from its design, independent of tuning: it
has one depth for the whole batch, so it cannot exploit the 7× per-sequence
acceptance spread the drafter exposes; and it reads only the acceptance side
of the trade, so a load swing that doubles the price of a verify slot without
changing acceptance is invisible to it.

## Appendix: the eager-mode ablation

We first brought the engine up with CUDA graphs disabled (`enforce_eager`),
and the resulting dataset is worth keeping because it shows how completely
the host can invert the economics. In eager mode the engine is
host-overhead-bound: a decode step costs ~36--50 ms nearly independent of
batch size, but realized serving cost still scales hard with verify width
(at batch 1, ~49 ms at width 3 vs ~105 ms at width 9 -- the growth living in
eager MoE dispatch, drafter catch-up, and rejection sampling rather than in
FLOPs or bytes). Under that regime *shallow speculation dominates
everything*: fixed $\gamma{=}2$ beats both vanilla $\gamma{=}8$ and
no-speculation by 35--55% at every concurrency we measured, and the
acceptance-EMA adapter -- which correctly observes that acceptance is healthy
and sits deep -- loses ~30% to fixed-2 while being right about acceptance.
Same model, same GPU, same drafter; flip one engine flag and the optimal
draft length moves by 4x and the failure mode of acceptance-only adaptation
flips sign. We can't think of a cleaner argument that draft-length policy
must read the cost side from the deployment itself.

## Appendix: where this analysis is thinnest

- **Acceptance is assumed independent of batch size.** Acceptance was
  measured once and composed with cost models that vary load. Nothing in the
  literature suggests batch-coupled acceptance, but we did not measure it.
  (Acceptance *did* transfer cleanly across datasets: the SPEED-Bench prior
  predicted ultrachat's realized committed-per-step within 2%.)
- **One model, one drafter, one temperature.** The calibration's shape
  (monotone, near-calibrated) is what the gate needs; its portability across
  model families is untested.
- **The simulator's cost model is a roofline, and rooflines are
  shape-blind.** The simulator reproduced fixed-γ orderings and located the
  gating wins, but it models a step's cost as a function of its contents,
  not its history. The real engine's two largest effects -- the per-boot
  graph-capture cliff and the shape-transition penalty -- are invisible to
  it, and both moved results by more than the effects it does model. This
  is an argument for the profiled/online cost path, not against simulation:
  the simulator answers "is the policy worth building"; only the deployment
  answers "what does it earn here."
- **Fixed-γ baselines truncate an 8-deep drafter.** A native fixed-k server
  would pay k drafter passes, not 8. The vanilla and no-spec arms are
  native; the fixed-2/4 arms carry the extra drafter cost, which flatters
  the adaptive arms slightly in comparisons against fixed-2/4 (but not
  against vanilla, the strongest static baseline in most regimes).
- **Single-GPU, single-node.** Expert-parallel deployments change the MoE
  cost structure (the companion post discusses how); the policies' inputs
  would need re-profiling there, which is rather the point.
