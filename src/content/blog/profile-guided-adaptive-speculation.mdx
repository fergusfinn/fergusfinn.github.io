Last [time](https://fergusfinn.com/blog/economics-of-speculative-decoding/) we
discussed the changing economics of speculative decoding. The strategy for
getting the most tokens out of a running model has become more complex as the
"market" for tokens in the running inference engine has become more dynamic. 

In this post, we want to figure out what the optimal strategy is for
speculating in a fast-changing environment.

## First, a new model

Let's swap the model from the last post, cause why not.
[Qwen3.6-35B-A3B](https://huggingface.co/Qwen/Qwen3.6-35B-A3B) is a hybrid
mixture-of-experts model from the Qwen team. 

The expert half is pretty much the same as we worked out for [DeepSeek
Flash](https://fergusfinn.com/blog/economics-of-speculative-decoding/#the-expert-tax).
Every layer routes each token to $8$[>1] of $256$ experts plus one shared expert, which
is the same coupon-collector picture from last time: at small batch each token
tends to drag in its own fresh experts and amortises almost nothing, and the
marginal token only rides resident experts for free once the batch size is
large enough to have triggered most of them.

[>1]: Contrast $6$ of $256$ for Deepseek Flash, the knee when all experts are
active, such as it is, arrives sooner.

The attention half is pretty different. Recent Qwen models have bet on 'hybrid
attention': mixing both novel linear attention mechanisms (specifically,
[GatedDeltaNet](https://arxiv.org/abs/2412.06464)) with traditional (GQA)
attention. Qwen alternates its layers three to one: thirty of the forty are
GatedDeltaNet linear-attention layers, and only ten are conventional full
attention[>2]. The result is that the result from last time -- that MLA becomes
compute bound when speculating -- doesn't apply, both the linear attention and
GQA layers both have an arithmetic intensity that doesn't saturate at any
reasonable draft length, so speculation keeps paying for long sequences.

[>2]: This is another path to KV cache compression, different from DeepSeek's
maybe more ambitious modifications of the standard attention mechanism.


So it comes down to: the expert tax from last time, plus an attention bill that
is quartered and, for most layers, flat in context. The full roofline maths is
in the [appendix](#appendix-rooflines-for-the-qwen-model).

What we've left out, as we did before, is the cost of *producing* the
speculated tokens.

## The changing face of speculation

There are two research threads that have changed how draft models are built:

One major boost in the performance of draft models has been to condition them
on richer outputs from the target model. Conditioning eases the training
objective of the speculator, making higher accept lengths easier to
achieve[>3]. 

[>3]: Conditioning on the hidden states has a drawback though, in that the speculator
must run in series with the target model (since it needs the hidden states in
order to run. Generally, hidden states from closer to the end of the speculator
model are more useful than those from the start, and the speculator can only
run once they're available). So there's little potential for overlap between
speculator and target. TODO: Even if there were, the right framing ought to be to
figure out the opportunity cost of those FLOPs -- for example, if we proposed
running the draft model asynchronously on another GPU (as in
[SSD](https://arxiv.org/abs/2603.03251)), we ought to treat the baseline as one
in which that GPU was used to power inference on the target model via wider TP or something?.

The other half of the story powering the step change in speculative decoding is
the hardware sympathy of the drafter.
[DFlash](https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.06036) makes use of the conditioning on the
hidden states to make diffusion, which has generally given poor performance for
pure text generation, work for speculation generation. The drafter workload is
then much closer to its ridge point, produces its own tokens much faster, and
the result is higher throughput for the same accept length.

Both factors are driving massive improvements in throughput [>4].

[>4]: See this great
[work](https://www.lmsys.org/blog/2026-06-15-next-generation-speculative-decoding-dflash-v2/)
from the modal, sglang, and z lab teams.

We discussed last time that there are two costs to pay during speculation: the
cost of the draft model, and the cost of the verify. We focussed on the cost of
the verify, and held the draft cost as a constant fraction of the target model.

This is bad modelling.

## The drafter has its own roofline

There are two different kinds of draft models in vogue at the moment.

The [MTP](https://arxiv.org/abs/2412.19437)[>F] head Qwen ships is a single transformer layer that drafts
autoregressively. To propose a draft of $\gamma$ tokens it runs $\gamma$ times
in sequence, each pass taking the last token and producing the next, each pass
a single layer followed by a projection through the $248{,}000$-entry
vocabulary. So the drafter's cost runs roughly linear in $\gamma$, the
vocabulary projection is paid $\gamma$ times over.

[>F]: Basically [EAGLE](https://arxiv.org/abs/2401.15077), but pretrained as
part of the model. Link is to deepseek which introduced doing this at pretrain
time.

DFlash is an eight-layer block-diffusion model that drafts a fixed block in one
forward pass, every position at once. In the setup here that block is $D=16$:
the weights stream once and the vocabulary projection runs once over the whole
block. A policy can choose to verify fewer than those positions, but it still
paid the draft cost of the full block.

Each has a cost to run at each point in batch-size and usable-depth space. MTP
pays for $\gamma$ serial one-token passes. DFlash pays for one fixed $16$-block
pass, then the policy chooses how much of that block to verify. At some points
in that space, DFlash is cheaper, at some points EAGLE/MTP is cheaper[>J]:

[>J]: This reasoning is irrespective of draft length, the actual wall clock win
comes from trading off forward pass cost vs. total accepted.

<DrafterCrossover client:load />

The consequence for speculation is that the drafter is not a fixed tax. MTP is
linear in the requested depth and its routed-expert residency grows with batch;
DFlash is flat in requested depth for a chosen block size, but not flat in batch.

Drafting against matched prompts at temperature $0.6$, the two heads commit
almost the same number of tokens per round, a little over three either way,
with DFlash ahead only on the predictable material and level on everything
harder.[>10]

[>10]: Matched collection on Qwen3.6-35B-A3B over [SPEED-Bench](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.09557), roughly thirty
thousand draft rounds each: MTP commits $3.27$ of $8$ drafted, DFlash $3.37$ of
$16$, with DFlash $+13$ to $+26\%$ on code, retrieval and prose and
level-or-behind on QA and high-entropy text. Details in the [acceptance
appendix](#appendix-acceptance-model-and-trace-provenance).

## A study in simulation

 To find out whether pricing the draft beats tuning it, this kind of cost model
 has to go inside an engine, with a scheduler, a queue, and a stream of
 requests that come and go.

I do that in a discrete-event simulator of a vLLM-shaped engine,
[inference-lab](https://github.com/doublewordai/inference-lab). It's designed
to split the difference between two different ways of experimenting with
inference systems. The pen and paper analytics gives a per-step cost but no
system: no batching, no queueing, no sense of load or data dependence. A real
engine has all of that, but it also carries confounds that are frustrating to
litigate case by case. Like boot-to-boot variation in which kernel shapes got
captured, or CUDA graphs, availability of specific GPU SKUs, &c. &c. 


In [inference-lab](https://github.com/doublewordai/inference-lab), each
scheduler step, a batch is composed from a facsimile of vLLM's scheduler logic
-- for aggregated, chunked prefill, we do their decode preference scheduling,
but we also support disaggregated prefill. Then, the simulator prices that
batch according to the modelling, and increments a timing counter. Then
eviction, token generation, admission, KV cache management, etc. kick in to
produce the next step. It's purely [event
driven](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Discrete-event_simulation), so its really
fast[>Q].

[>Q]: It also compiles to wasm, so you can do fun stuff like this:
https://inference-lab.doubleword.ai/.

The idea is, if an idea is right, what could a perfect implementation achieve,
unconstrained by the difficulty of implementation.

We want to understand what would happen in a real engine. We could just measure
$\alpha$, the average accepted speculation tokens for a real engine and
calibrate the simulator from i.i.d. acceptance. Beyond just global acceptance
though, there are real signals being emitted per draft round -- as an example,
the logprobs of the drafter can be used to calibrate the size of the
verification. So really, we need real calibration data. So we run real end to
end experiments on modal with the model and various drafters, to get banks of
acceptance rates per position, see the [appendix]].

## The headroom, and where the implementation challenges are

### How to pick a draft length

When we pick a draft length $\gamma$, we're taking a bet: we pay in wall clock
time to produce and verify $\gamma$ speculated tokens for the whole batch, and
get back however many the target accepts. The right bet maximises total
committed output per unit time, which is just steady-state throughput. Let
$C(B,\gamma)$ be the wall-clock cost of the whole scheduler step for a batch of
$B$ decode sequences, and let $\mathbb{E}[\text{accepted}\mid\gamma]$ be the
per-sequence accepted draft tokens. Then a homogeneous draft depth has

$$
\mathrm{goodput}_B(\gamma) =
\frac{B\left(\mathbb{E}[\text{accepted} \mid \gamma] + 1\right)}{C(B, \gamma)}.
$$

At a fixed batch size the factor of $B$ does not change the chosen $\gamma$, but
it does matter for reading the quantity as throughput. Each step the policy
takes $\gamma^\star = \arg\max_\gamma \mathrm{goodput}_B(\gamma)$, over
$\gamma$ from $0$ (don't speculate) up to the drafter's usable depth.

This is basically just the speedup objective from [last
time](https://fergusfinn.com/blog/economics-of-speculative-decoding/). The
numerator is the average number of tokens that actually commit per sequence at
each depth[>F]. The denominator is the real roofline cost of the whole step: the
expert tax and the hybrid-attention bill for the live batch of $B$ sequences
each verifying $\gamma+1$ tokens, plus the drafter charged according to its
shape: $\gamma$ serial passes for MTP, or the fixed block pass for DFlash.


[>F]: We did average out $\alpha_k$ per position (i.e. acceptance rate after
$1$ token, after $2$ tokens etc.). Turns out we needn't have bothered, and
$\alpha_k \sim \alpha^k$ pretty closely.

would. For each concurrency, the best fixed $\gamma$ is the one you would have
chosen if you knew that exact operating point ahead of time. It does!

### Heterogeneous draft lengths: gating on confidence

The priced policy so far answers the same for the whole batch: under the global
average acceptance curve, what single draft depth maximises goodput. The number
of committed tokens is not clustered around the mean, but more like
all-or-nothing. On a given round the drafter tends to be right about almost
everything it drafts or wrong almost at once. The average hides some interesting
shape. Rounds are mostly 'near-misses' or 'clean sweeps':

i.e. both kinds of drafters have a spike at their max committed tokens. (TODO:
is this spike just the integral of like a fitted curve past the max off to

A single depth has to compromise
between those two populations, deep enough to cash in the easy rounds, shallow
enough not to verify the hard ones into the ground.

Happily, the drafter has an opinion about which kind of round it is before the
verifier runs. Plot the cumulative product of its per-depth confidences, against
the length that actually committed. Diagonals are 'perfectly calibrated': i.e.
the empirical accepted tokens at that length is the same as the confidence
derived from the drafter logprobs. Below the diagonal, the drafter was
overconfident, above, not confident enough.

<AcceptJointHeatmap client:load />

Write round $i$'s expected committed tokens at depth $g$ from its own per-depth
confidences $a_k^i$,

$$
m_i(g) = 1 + \sum_{d=1}^{g} \prod_{k=1}^{d} a_k^i .
$$

The homogeneous policy constrains the whole batch to one depth and maximises
once,

$$
g^\star =
\arg\max_g
\frac{\sum_i m_i(g)}{C(B, g\mathbf{1})}.
$$

For per-sequence gating, the honest object is the vector of depths
$\mathbf{g} = (g_1,\ldots,g_B)$:

$$
\mathbf{g}^\star =
\arg\max_{\mathbf{g}}
\frac{\sum_i m_i(g_i)}{C(B, \mathbf{g})}.
$$

That cost does not decompose by sequence. MoE expert residency is
coupon-collected across the batch, attention and recurrent-state reads are
shared scheduler-step costs, and DFlash still pays the fixed block even when the
verify is shallow. So the simulator prices the whole vector, not an independent
$m_i(g)/C(g)$ decision per row.

There's a signal that we could use if we wanted to. Running the simulation
tells us what that signal could be worth:

<GatingLadder client:load />

So there are big gains at small batch size: where the expert coupon-collector
makes mis-speculation expensive. And then, as parts of the model go past the
compute bound cliff, there are gains from acceptance gating, since speculation
now only wins in those parts of the model that are compute bound. The
batch-averaged signal washes out as the batch size increases -- i.e, you can't
adaptively choose a global $\gamma$ from the confidences -- because the larger
batch is more heterogeneous.

## What to build

To make it real, you have to figure out how to build this policy in real time.
There are a couple of issues:

1. **You don't know $\alpha_k^i$**. This one's pretty easy - you just do an EMA
   estimate of the target acceptance rate over time, or use the drafter
   confidence.
2. **You don't know $C$**. This ones much harder. You can just use the roofline
   model - since hopefully, the performance in the real engine is at least
   correlated with the modelled performance, you can pick max $\gamma$ from max
   modelled $\gamma$ without going too far off-policy. You can also build a
   model of the real engine on real hardware, pointwise with batch size,
   $\gamma$, either online EMA or with a profiling step. 

Once you've done that what do you get? Several things:

1. For homogeneous workloads: you don't ever have to tune the speculation
   length, and so speculation can be enabled by default. 'Configuration tax' is
   a real thing in inference engines: no matter what features you add to push
   performance, they're useless unless people can find and enable them, and
   even AI coding agents aren't very good at [doing
   that](https://inferencebench.ai/).
2. There's something like a ~10-30% throughput win at various operating points
   by picking $\gamma_i$ dynamically per sequence, based on a cost model and
   drafter confidence -- provided already by existing models.
3. There's a win across the board for heterogeneous, bursty, dynamic workloads.
   If your system lives between two operating points (i.e. half at concurrency
   $1024$, half at concurrency $128$), any static $\gamma$ will be mistuned.
   Even better, you can use the dynamism you've afforded yourself to accept
   more flexible workloads: switch the same deployment from [Claude fast
   mode](https://code.claude.com/docs/en/fast-mode) to regular speed just by
   increasing batch size, without having to relaunch or reconfigure how you're
   doing speculation.
   
It's gonna be hard to build though. The benefit of simulation is you don't have
to think about implementation difficulty, the drawback is you haven't thought
about implementation difficulty. If you can run speculation at any static
length up to $\gamma_\mathrm{max}$, you need to figure out how to do CUDA
graphs for each speculative shape. If you want the wins from ragged $\gamma_i$,
your engine needs to be able to efficiently raggedly speculate. Estimators need
to be updated online, accurately, account for hysteresis, outliers, warmups,
JIT compilation, etc. Plus, in the model I've picked here, speculation has some
gnarly complications: rewinding linear layers when you mis-speculate is not a
simple KV-truncate operation[>E].

[>E]: Some nice work on making this easier: https://dao-lab.ai/blog/2026/replayssm/

But, on the balance of the simulation evidence at least, its worth it. 

## Appendix: Rooflines for the Qwen model

Everything here is Qwen3.6-35B-A3B. The expert term the same as [last
time](https://fergusfinn.com/blog/economics-of-speculative-decoding/#the-expert-tax):
coupon-collector routing, $k=8$ of $E=256$, knee at $E/k=32$. The
attention is where the model differs.

The ten full-attention layers behave in the standard attention way. They are
grouped-query[>5], and the KV cache is re-read on every decode step and grows
linearly with context. So the arithmetic intensity of the standard attention
layers goes as $\sim 8T$, with $T$ the verify length.

[>5]: $16$ query heads, $2$ key/value heads, head dimension $256$

The thirty linear layers do not keep a growing cache at all. A GatedDeltaNet
layer carries a fixed-size recurrent state, a small matrix per head[>6] that it
updates in place as it consumes each token[>7]. The state summarises the whole
history in constant space, so there is nothing that grows with context to
re-read. Across the thirty layers that state comes to roughly 33 megabytes per
sequence, re-read every decode step no matter how long the sequence is.

[>6]: 128 by 128, across 32 value heads

[>7]: GatedDeltaNet is a gated form of the delta rule: each token writes a
rank-one update into the state and a gate sets how fast old memory decays. At
decode it runs as a constant-work recurrence, which is why its cost does not
grow with context.

Those two facts set up a crossover with no analogue in a pure-attention model.
At short context the fixed $33$ megabytes of recurrent state dominate the
attention-memory bill, and the growing KV cache of the ten full-attention
layers only overtakes it past about $1,600$ tokens of context. Below that, the
cost of attending is essentially context-independent; above it, the bill still
climbs, but on only a quarter of the layers, so it climbs four times more
slowly than the dense-attention intuition suggests.

### The full-attention layers (ten of forty)

These are ordinary softmax attention, so last post's arithmetic-intensity model
applies. For $T$ verify tokens over $S$ context tokens,

$$
\mathrm{AI} = \frac{f\,T S}{m_c\,S + m_q\,T},
$$

with $f$ the FLOPs per query-context pair, $m_c$ the bytes read per context
token, and $m_q$ the bytes per query token. The layers are grouped-query
($n_h = 16$ query heads, $n_\text{kv} = 2$ key/value heads, head dimension
$d = 256$), and Qwen is not trained for an fp8 cache, so KV stays in bf16
($b_\text{kv} = 2$) or we pay an accuracy cost:

$$
f = 4\,n_h d = 16{,}384, \qquad
m_c = 2\,n_\text{kv}\,d\,b_\text{kv} = 2048\ \text{bytes}, \qquad
m_q = n_h\,d\,b_q .
$$

At decode the KV read dominates the denominator ($m_c S \gg m_q T$), so the
intensity rises with context to the plateau

$$
\mathrm{AI} \to \frac{f\,T}{m_c} = \frac{2\,n_h}{n_\text{kv}\,b_\text{kv}}\,T = 8\,T .
$$

Against a B200's bf16 ridge of $\approx 281$ these layers stay memory-bound
until $T \approx 35$. That is the difference from last post's MLA, which crossed
the ridge at $T = 2$: on grouped-query attention a speculated token is close to
free for any draft length we would use, and paid on only a quarter of the
layers.

### The GatedDeltaNet layers (thirty of forty)

Per layer, there is fixed state and per-token recurrence over it.
The state $K^\top V$ is a $d_k \times d_v$ matrix per value head, read and
updated once per step:

$$
m_\text{state} = n_v\,d_k\,d_v\,b_\text{state} = 32 \cdot 128 \cdot 128 \cdot 2
\approx 1.0\ \text{MB per layer},
$$

independent of $S$. The full simulator state also includes the small convolution
state, which is why the total below is $33.4$ MB rather than exactly
$30 \times 1.0$ MB. The compute is the gated delta-rule update plus the read-out:

$$
f_\text{rec} = c\,n_v\,d_k\,d_v \quad \text{per token}, \qquad c \approx 8\ \text{FLOPs},
$$

counting the rank-one write $\beta\,v k^\top$, the delta correction
$\beta\,k(k^\top S)$, and the output $o = S^\top q$ (the exact constant follows
from the GatedDeltaNet update equations). The intensity is the ratio,

$$
\mathrm{AI}_\text{gdn} = \frac{f_\text{rec}\,T}{m_\text{state}} =
\frac{c}{b_\text{state}}\,T = 4\,T,
$$

with no $S$ in it. 

### What the two share, and where they part

For speculation, it works out the same: both layers amortise a per-sequence
memory read over verify width $T$. Softmax amortises the KV read, GatedDeltaNet
the state read, and in both the intensity is proportional to $T$, so a wider
draft buys more compute per byte moved. Neither intensity falls with context
either, the softmax one rises with $S$ towards $8T$ and the linear one sits
flat at $4T$.

The difference is the maintained state. Per sequence the attention-memory read
per step for Qwen-35b-a3b is:

$$
m(S) = \underbrace{20{,}480\,S}_{\text{full-attn KV}} +
\underbrace{33.4\times10^6}_{\text{GDN state}}\ \text{bytes},
\qquad
S^\star = \frac{33.4\times10^6}{20{,}480} \approx 1{,}600\ \text{tokens}.
$$

Below $S^\star$ the fixed GatedDeltaNet state dominates the read; above it the
dense-attention model does. Either way the attention side of this model barely
resists speculation. What resists it is the expert tax, and the drafter.

## Appendix: the drafter roofline

The verifier appendix priced what it costs to *check* a speculated token. This
prices what it costs to *produce* one, and it is the cleaner of the two pictures
because both drafters reduce to one object: the output projection. Qwen3.6's
hidden width is $d = 2{,}048$ and its vocabulary is $V = 248{,}320$, so the
output projection is separate from the input embedding and is

$$
P_\text{head} = d\,V = 2{,}048 \times 248{,}320 \approx 0.509\ \text{billion params}
= 1.02\ \text{GB at bf16}.
$$

That is larger than anything else either head touches, and the entire contrast
between the two drafters is how many times each one pays to read it.

### MTP

The MTP head is a single decoder layer: an EAGLE-style $2d \to d$ fusion
projection, grouped-query attention ($\approx 18.9$ M params), and the layer's
MoE MLP. It drafts autoregressively: a draft of $\gamma$ tokens is $\gamma$
forward passes in series, one token produced per pass.

The dense part read on every pass is the output head plus attention and fusion,
$P_\text{dense} \approx 0.536$ B. The active expert work adds $(k+1)w$ params,
with $k=8$ routed experts, one shared expert, and $w=3{,}145{,}728$ params per
expert, so the active per-token pass is still
$P_\text{act} \approx 0.564$ B. But the resident expert read grows with batch:

$$
e(B) = E\left(1 - \left(1 - \frac1E\right)^{Bk}\right), \qquad
P_\text{res}(B) = P_\text{dense} + (1 + e(B))w .
$$

So its per-pass roofline is

$$
\mathrm{AI}_\text{MTP}(B) = \frac{P_\text{act}\,B}{P_\text{res}(B)},
$$

which is close to $B$ only while the resident expert set is still small. Across
the batch range in the chart it remains below the B200 bf16 ridge of
$\approx 281$, so the weight read dominates and the cost of a $\gamma$-deep draft
is

$$
t_\text{MTP}(\gamma, B) =
\gamma \cdot \max\!\left(\frac{2\,P_\text{act}\,B}{\text{peak}},\ \frac{2\,P_\text{res}(B)}{\text{BW}}\right).
$$

At $B=1$ this recovers the simple $\gamma \times 141\,\mu\text{s}$ estimate. At
larger $B$, the coupon-collected routed experts raise the memory term even while
the active compute remains sparse. It is still linear in $\gamma$.

### DFlash

DFlash is an eight-layer block-diffusion drafter. Its layers are dense (SwiGLU
width $6{,}144$, grouped-query attention with $32$ query and $4$ key/value
heads), so the eight come to $\approx 0.45$ B, and a small fusion that ingests
hidden states from five of the target's layers ($5 \times 2048 \to 2048$) adds
another $\approx 0.02$ B. It carries no head of its own either, borrowing the
target's, so $P_\text{DF} = 0.47\,\text{B} + P_\text{head} \approx 0.98$ B. The
deployed head here drafts a fixed $D=16$ block in one forward pass. Choosing a
shallower $\gamma$ changes the verifier width, not the draft pass, so $BD$
positions ride that one read:

$$
\mathrm{AI}_\text{DFlash} = \frac{2\,P_\text{DF}\,BD}{2\,P_\text{DF}} = BD,
\qquad
t_\text{DFlash}(B) =
\max\!\left(\frac{2\,P_\text{DF}\,BD}{\text{peak}},\ \frac{2\,P_\text{DF}}{\text{BW}}\right), \quad D=16.
$$

At decode ($B = 1$) the block does not cross the ridge until $D \approx 281$,
so the read dominates for every depth one would ever draft and the cost is

$$
t_\text{DFlash}(1) \approx \frac{1.96\ \text{GB}}{8\ \text{TB/s}} \approx 245\ \mu\text{s},
$$

flat in the requested verify depth $\gamma$ for this fixed block size.

## Appendix: acceptance model and trace provenance

Acceptance numbers in the post come from real draft rounds.The banks were
collected by running Qwen3.6-35B-A3B (on modal) as the target with the native
one-layer MTP head and DFlash, over
[SPEED-Bench](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.09557) at temperature $0.6$, and
logging every speculation round.

A round records two things together. The first is the drafter's own per-depth
confidence, read off its draft-time logprobs. The second is the outcome, the
number of tokens the target actually committed that round.

First: how many tokens get accepted? On a log axis a single $\alpha^k$ is a
straight line, so the bend in each measured curve is its distance from
geometric: both decay faster than geometric early, and DFlash's deep tail is
heavier than geometric, not lighter.

## Appendix: model and drafter configs

### Verifier

[Qwen3.6-35B-A3B](https://huggingface.co/Qwen/Qwen3.6-35B-A3B):
3 linear layers then 1 full-attention layer, repeated: 30
  [GatedDeltaNet](https://arxiv.org/abs/2412.06464) linear and 10 GQA
  full-attention; every layer MoE
  ($256$ routed $+ 1$ shared, $8$ per token); hidden $2048$, vocab $248{,}320$;
  bf16 weights and KV.

### Drafters

Both use to the target's $0.509$ B vocab head rather than shipping their own.

| | [MTP](https://arxiv.org/abs/2412.19437) | [DFlash](https://huggingface.co/z-lab/Qwen3.6-35B-A3B-DFlash) |
|---|---|---|
| Drafter | autoregressive, 1 MoE layer + EAGLE fusion ($\approx 56$ M active) | block-diffusion, 8 dense SwiGLU-$6144$ layers + 5-layer fusion ($\approx 474$ M) |
| Draft | $\gamma$ tokens over $\gamma$ passes | fixed $16$-block, one pass; verify may truncate |
| Streamed per pass | $\approx 0.564$ B active, dense head re-read each pass, routed experts coupon-collect with $B$ | $\approx 0.983$ B, head read once |

### Hardware

One B200, TP1/EP1: bf16 $2.25$, fp8 $4.5$, fp4 $9.0$ PFLOP/s; $8$ TB/s HBM; $192$ GB.

### Experiments

The two figures in *The headroom* are closed-loop sweeps over one setup:
decode-only (ISL $1$, OSL $1024$, so prefill is disaggregated), a single request
size, concurrency $1$ to $2048$ in powers of two, fixed seed. Acceptance is
replayed from the banks above, one round per decode step, so it carries the real
per-round variation rather than a single $\alpha$.

The first sweep pits the best fixed draft length, chosen by simulating the e2e
benchmark at every $\gamma$ and picking the best in hindsight, against the
priced policy run once choosing live. The second takes that priced policy as a
baseline and adds the confidence signal in steps: one verify width for the
whole batch, then a width per sequence, then the same allocation handed the
true accept pattern instead of the confidence (the oracle). The code and the
banks are in [inference-lab](https://github.com/doublewordai/inference-lab).
