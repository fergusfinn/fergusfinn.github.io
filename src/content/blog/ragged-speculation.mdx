Last [time](https://fergusfinn.com/blog/adaptive-speculation/) we built a priced
policy that chooses a draft length $\gamma$ at runtime. But it answers the same
for the whole batch: under the global average acceptance curve, what single draft
depth maximises goodput. That leaves something on the table, because the batch is
not homogeneous.

## Gating draft depth on confidence

The number of committed tokens is not clustered around the mean, but more like
all-or-nothing. On a given round the drafter tends to be right about almost
everything it drafts or wrong almost at once. The average hides some interesting
shape. Rounds are mostly 'near-misses' or 'clean sweeps':

i.e. both kinds of drafters have a spike at their max committed tokens. (TODO:

A single depth has to compromise
between those two populations, deep enough to cash in the easy rounds, shallow
enough not to verify the hard ones into the ground.

Happily, the drafter has an opinion about which kind of round it is before the
verifier runs. Plot the cumulative product of its per-depth confidences, against
the length that actually committed. Diagonals are 'perfectly calibrated': i.e.
the empirical accepted tokens at that length is the same as the confidence
derived from the drafter logprobs. Below the diagonal, the drafter was
overconfident, above, not confident enough.

<AcceptJointHeatmap client:load />

Write round $i$'s expected committed tokens at depth $\gamma$ from its own per-depth
confidences $a_k^i$,

$$
m_i(\gamma) = 1 + \sum_{d=1}^{\gamma} \prod_{k=1}^{d} a_k^i .
$$

The homogeneous policy constrains the whole batch to one depth and maximises
once,

$$
\gamma^\star =
\arg\max_\gamma
\frac{\sum_i m_i(\gamma)}{C(B, \gamma\mathbf{1})}.
$$

Really you can do better though, since the confidences are per sequence in the
batch. For per-sequence gating, the object is the vector of depths
$\mathbf{\gamma} = (\gamma_1,\ldots,\gamma_B)$:

$$
\mathbf{\gamma}^\star =
\arg\max_{\mathbf{\gamma}}
\frac{\sum_i m_i(\gamma_i)}{C(B, \mathbf{\gamma})}.
$$

There's a signal that we could use if we wanted to. Running the simulation
tells us what that signal could be worth:

Perfect calibration is the case where we give the policy oracle information:
i.e. it only ever drafts things that it knows ahead of time will be accepted.

So there are big gains at small batch size: where the expert coupon-collector
makes mis-speculation expensive. And then, as parts of the model go past the
compute bound cliff, there are gains from acceptance gating, since speculation
now only wins in those parts of the model that are compute bound. The
batch-averaged signal washes out as the batch size increases -- i.e, you can't
adaptively choose a global $\gamma$ from the confidences -- because the larger
batch is more heterogeneous.

There's something like a ~10-30% throughput win at various operating points by
picking $\gamma_i$ dynamically per sequence, based on a cost model and drafter
confidence -- provided already by existing models. If you want those wins,
though, your engine needs to be able to efficiently raggedly speculate.

## Speculation as a QoS knob

Everything above spends the per-sequence draft depth to maximise *aggregate*
goodput: draft deep where the tokens will be accepted, shallow where they won't.
But the same ragged mechanism answers a different question just as well. Within
one batch different requests can want different things -- a latency-sensitive
request wants its tokens as fast as possible, a bulk request is happy to trade
latency for throughput -- and per-sequence draft depth is a knob on exactly that
tradeoff. Spending depth to maximise goodput and spending it to hit a latency
target are not the same allocation, and where they disagree is the interesting
part.
