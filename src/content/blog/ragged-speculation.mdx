Building the [last post](https://fergusfinn.com/blog/adaptive-speculation/) meant
collecting a fair amount of data about how our two draft models behave: half a
million draft rounds each, every round recording the drafter's own per-depth
confidence and the number of tokens the target actually committed (published as
[`qwen3.6-specdec-calibration`](https://huggingface.co/datasets/Doubleword/qwen3.6-specdec-calibration)).
Alongside it, we logged which experts every token routed through.

Here are a couple of stories from that data. First, from the routing: how width
(batching) and depth (speculation) trade off against one another, and when each
wins. Then, from the acceptance side: why speculating a different amount for each
request beats picking one depth for the whole batch.

Let's take the depth axis first. Here's a fun interview question. Imagine you're
running Qwen3.6-35B-A3B at batch size $1$, on a single GPU, and you decide you
want to increase throughput. For whatever reason, your engine can only work on
$2$ tokens at a time. You have two choices[>1]:

[>1]: Assume you don't have to prefill anything to batch, and that the
sequences are short enough that the KV cache movement doesn't come into play.

1. Run at batch size $2$ by batching $2$ random user sequences together.
2. Running at batch size $1$, speculate out to draft length $2$, with a
   per-token acceptance rate $\alpha$.

Which is better, assuming you only care about the number of tokens being output
per second (not TPOT, i.e. you don't care about tokens per user per second)? In
a perfectly implemented inference engine, which gives you the maximum output
throughput?

Here's a sensible answer.

> Assuming that everything is memory bound, batching is always better for
> $\alpha<1$, because there's no chance that a token added to the working set
> by increasing the batch size will be rejected. You'd only choose speculative
> decoding in this case if you wanted to improve the TPOT for your user at the
> expense of some global throughput. At $\alpha=1$, they have the same
> throughput.

Here's something that's true sometimes:

> Speculating 2 positions out produces, globally, more output tokens per
> second, than running at a batch size of 2, even with $\alpha=0.9$.

Here's why.

## MoEs are weird

First, let's look at the distribution of routed experts (previously, we've just
assumed uniform routing, so we get [coupon-collector maths](https://fergusfinn.com/blog/economics-of-speculative-decoding/#the-expert-tax)):

Surprisingly non-uniform! The distribution is kinda
[Zipfian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zipf%27s_law) -- a handful of experts
take many times their share of the tokens, and a long tail barely fires.

More interesting though: are there patterns in which tokens activate which
experts? In particular, are tokens next to each other in a sequence ("My",
"cat") likely to co-activate the same experts more than random
sequences?

<WidthVsDepth client:load />

The chart counts the distinct experts one verify forward touches as $N$ grows,
three ways: $N$ separate sequences (width), one sequence speculating $N$ (depth),
and the uniform coupon-collector we'd been assuming. The batch sits below
coupon-collector because of the skew above -- real sequences keep hitting the
same popular experts -- and the run sits below the batch again, because adjacent
tokens co-activate.

So it resolves the interview question. At batch size 1 we're memory-bound, and
verifying two speculated tokens moves less expert weight than adding a second
sequence would. Even throwing away 10% of speculated tokens at $\alpha = 0.9$,
depth beats width.

## Confidence gating

Routing wasn't the only thing we'd been treating as uniform. The batch is not
homogeneous. There are many reasons we should treat each request in the batch
differently. The most prominent: each decode request is
not equally easy to speculate from. If we can get some sense of how confident
the drafter is, we can use that to calibrate how many tokens we should propose
to the verifier, and save verifier compute.

I wrote the skeleton & did the simulations for this part a week ago. The
conclusion I came to was that this was a good idea but that I wouldn't want to
have to implement it. Happily, [DeepSeek](https://arxiv.org/abs/2606.19348)
came along with DSpark, and did it way better than I likely would have, along
with some great modelling of how adding causality to [DFlash](https://arxiv.org/abs/2602.06036) style drafters
increases their ability to produce great drafts. The results show great
throughput gains, and also that the naive confidence calibration (just reading
off the logits) that this post argues for leaves performance on the table.

I think the argument and the data is still interesting though, so here's the
argument in close to its original form:

The number of committed tokens is not clustered around the mean, but more like
all-or-nothing. On a given round the drafter tends to be right about almost
everything it drafts or wrong almost at once. The average hides some interesting
shape. Rounds are mostly 'near-misses' or 'clean sweeps':

<AcceptLengthHist client:load />

i.e. both kinds of drafters have a spike at their max committed tokens.[>2]

[>2]: One good question for those training draft models: is this spike at the
maximum draft length representative of the fact that the model could have gone
further, but wasn't able to?

A single fixed depth has to compromise between those two populations, deep
enough to cash in the easy rounds, shallow enough not to waste too much time on
requests that will be almost all rejections.

The drafter already has an opinion about which kind of round it is before the
verifier runs. Below, we plot the cumulative product of the drafter's per-depth
confidences, against the length that actually committed. By confidence we mean
the probability corresponding to the accepted token. Diagonals are 'perfectly
calibrated': i.e. the empirical accepted tokens at that length is the same as
the confidence derived from the drafter logprobs. Below the diagonal, the
drafter was overconfident, above, not confident enough.

<AcceptJointHeatmap client:load />

The argument is that there's lots of blue along the diagonal, and that's free
signal we could be using to improve our drafting policy.

How can we do that?

Write round $i$'s expected committed tokens at depth $\gamma$ from its own
per-depth confidences $a_k^i$,

$$
m_i(\gamma) = 1 + \sum_{d=1}^{\gamma} \prod_{k=1}^{d} a_k^i .
$$

The homogeneous policy constrains the whole batch to one depth and maximises
once -- committed tokens over $C(B,\gamma)$, the wall-clock [cost of the
step](https://fergusfinn.com/blog/adaptive-speculation/):

$$
\gamma^\star =
\arg\max_\gamma
\frac{\sum_i m_i(\gamma)}{C(B, \gamma\mathbf{1})}.
$$

The confidence gating idea is that you can do better, since the confidences are
per sequence in the batch. For per-sequence gating, the object is the vector of
depths $\mathbf{\gamma} = (\gamma_1,\ldots,\gamma_B)$:

$$
\mathbf{\gamma}^\star =
\arg\max_{\mathbf{\gamma}}
\frac{\sum_i m_i(\gamma_i)}{C(B, \mathbf{\gamma})}.
$$

So at each step, we calculate the optimal $\bf{\gamma}$ vector, by
appropriately discounting future tokens based on how unlikely they are under
the distribution of the draft model. Running the [simulations](https://github.com/doublewordai/inference-lab) with $\bf{\gamma}$
picked in this way:

Perfect calibration is the case where we give the policy oracle information:
i.e. it only ever drafts things that it knows ahead of time will be accepted.

So there are big gains at small batch size: where the expert coupon-collector
makes mis-speculation expensive. And then, as parts of the model go past the
[compute bound cliff](https://fergusfinn.com/blog/economics-of-speculative-decoding/#the-expert-tax), there are gains from acceptance gating, since speculation
now only wins in those parts of the model that are compute bound. The
batch-averaged signal washes out as the batch size increases -- i.e, you can't
adaptively choose a global $\gamma$ from the confidences -- because the larger
batch is more heterogeneous, so you have to do it ragged.

There's something like a ~10-30% throughput win at various operating points by
picking $\gamma_i$ dynamically per sequence, based on a cost model and drafter
confidence -- provided already by existing models. If you want those wins,
though, your engine needs to be able to efficiently raggedly speculate. vLLM &
SGLang can't do this right now (though DSpark has lit a fire under the
community, since they showed the benefits of it).

## Speculation as a Quality-of-Service knob

Everything above spends the per-sequence draft depth to maximise _aggregate_
goodput. But one nice thing about being able to raggedly speculate is that it
opens up many more doors.

We've discussed optimizing for total goodput here, and its a sensible default.
But lots of inference providers (most interestingly
[Doubleword](https://doubleword.ai), obviously, but also [Claude
fast](https://code.claude.com/docs/en/fast-mode)) offer multiple
Quality-Of-Service tiers, allowing users to tradeoff time per output token per
request for a lower price (at large, a proxy for global throughput, since TPOT
doesn't track linearly with price). Usually you can't maintain very strict TPOT
guarantees while simultaneously running at large batch size (as you'd like to
if, for example, you want to co-run batch requests in your fast-mode servers).
But with better & more precise speculation - you can allocate your speculation
budget across your global batch size, such that your high QoS requests progress
faster, and your global throughput ought to be higher than you could maintain
without co-scheduling.

## Conclusion

Speculative decoding really is [all you
need](https://modal.com/blog/spec-is-all-u-need): there's very little in the
space of inference optimization that offers close to the throughput gains we
can get from well tuned speculators.

Modern LLMs at decode time are almost designed to be hard on the hardware --
MoEs, Attention, Autoregressive sampling, all kill our ability to drive the
right number of FLOPs through the hardware. The problems not likely to go away
soon: we're hitting the [memory
wall](https://newsletter.semianalysis.com/p/the-memory-wall) again (this time,
at much higher speed), and the logic of training makes it reasonable to design
our models with headroom to soak up as many FLOPs as possible.

This realisation of the outsized value of speculative decoding is gathering
pace: so fast that ideas that were live and well a week ago are outdated today.
