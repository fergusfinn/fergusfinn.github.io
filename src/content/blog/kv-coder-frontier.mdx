In the [last post](/blog/kv-entropy-coder) we introduced speculative KV
coding: compress a target model's KV cache losslessly by running a cheaper
predictor model on the same prompt on both sides of the wire, and arithmetic
coding the true cache against the predictor's output. With the FP8
quantisation of the target as the predictor, the cache codes at about 6.3
bits per scalar instead of 16, and about 2.1 bits per element for
FP8-native caches.

A skeptical reader will have noticed something, though: every place you
would want this, there is a do-nothing alternative that transfers zero
bits. The decoder holds the prompt and the weights. It can always just
*re-run prefill* and reconstruct the cache exactly, paying one target
forward pass and no bandwidth at all[>1]. Speculative KV coding only earns its
keep if the predictor is meaningfully cheaper than the target, because the
scheme's real cost is not the bits, it's the predictor's forward pass on
the decode side. And the FP8-of-target predictor is *not* meaningfully
cheaper: on FP8-capable hardware it costs about half a bf16 prefill.
Paying 0.5 prefills plus 6.3 bits per scalar to avoid paying 1.0 prefills
is a real saving, but it is not the regime that makes anyone redesign a
serving stack.

[>1]: Modulo bit-identical determinism of the prefill itself, which is the same requirement our scheme already carries for the predictor. See the first post.

So the object of study should not be a compression ratio. It should be the
**bits-versus-predictor-FLOPs frontier**: for each predictor budget $x$
(measured as a fraction of one bf16 target prefill), what is the best
achievable lossless rate? The two trivial endpoints are free: at $x = 0$,
code the raw cache against its per-channel statistics (10.3 b/s for
Qwen3-8B); at $x = 1$, re-prefill and send nothing. Published speculative
KV coding is one interior point, at roughly $(0.5, 6.3)$. The interesting
question, and the one this post attacks, is what the curve looks like at
small $x$. That is where "a very small model codes for a very large one"
either becomes real or dies.

## What the cross-model attempts taught us

The obvious way to get a cheap predictor is a smaller model from the same
family. Our attempts so far have not landed it: a per-layer linear bridge
from Qwen3-4B's KV onto Qwen3-8B's KV codes at 12.0 b/s (worse than no
predictor under the same coder), and a first pass at training the 4B with
LoRA jointly with the bridge under coding-length loss reaches 9.5 b/s,
against the FP8-of-target's 6.3 at the same cost. These are first
attempts with shallow adapters and small calibration sets, not a ceiling.
But the *shape* of where they struggle is informative about what to build
next.

First, K. Two independently trained models encode their K directions as
essentially unrelated per-head rotations. Qwen3's QK-norm constrains K
onto a sphere, and a bridge has to learn a sphere-to-sphere rotation per
head per layer before it can start paying for anything else. That is a
lot of budget spent recovering something the target's own weights give
you for free.

Second, V. The smaller model's late-layer V residual grows monotonically
with depth, and feeding the bridge the source's neighbouring layers does
not fix it: whatever produces the target's late V, the small model's
forward pass does not surface it in a linearly accessible form.

Both difficulties evaporate if the predictor shares the target's weights.
That does not close the cross-model door (a deeper bridge trained on more
data is still an open line), but it makes the weight-sharing predictor
the natural next rung on the ladder.

## The target's own first $k$ layers

Run the target's layers $0..k{-}1$ on the prompt, plus the (cheap) KV
projections of layer $k$. Two things fall out.

The first is almost embarrassing: the KV cache for layers $0..k$ is now
**bit-identical** to the target's, because we ran the target's own
computation. Those layers cost zero bits. No prediction, no residual, no
coder. A fraction $(k{+}1)/L$ of the cache is simply *recomputed* rather
than transferred, at a fraction $k/L$ of the prefill FLOPs.

The second is a feature vector: the layer-$k$ hidden state $h_k$, the
actual residual stream the target would use to compute every deeper
layer's KV. Per deeper layer, fit a closed-form linear probe from $h_k$ to
that layer's cached K and V (K probed in un-rotated RoPE space, since both
sides know positions). This is the predictor family: exact prefix plus
linear read-out, with one knob $k$ that sweeps the frontier.

Results for Qwen3-8B (36 layers), canonical coder, whole-cache rates:

| $k$ | $x$ (FLOPs) | b/s | vs anchors |
| :--- | ---: | ---: | :--- |
| 0  | 0.04 | 9.91 | x=0 anchor is 10.33 |
| 2  | 0.10 | 9.17 | |
| 4  | 0.15 | 8.58 | |
| 6  | 0.20 | 8.01 | |
| 9  | 0.28 | 7.13 | |
| 12 | 0.36 | **6.28** | FP8 anchor's rate at 72% of its cost |
| 18 | 0.52 | 4.54 | |
| 24 | 0.68 | 2.87 | |

And for Qwen3-32B (64 layers), where the published FP8 anchor sits at
$(0.5, 5.98)$:

| $k$ | $x$ (FLOPs) | b/s |
| :--- | ---: | ---: |
| 0  | 0.02 | 10.06 |
| 2  | 0.05 | 9.64 |
| 4  | 0.08 | 9.29 |
| 8  | 0.14 | 8.63 |
| 11 | 0.19 | 8.11 |
| 16 | 0.27 | 7.27 |
| 21 | 0.34 | 6.45 |
| 32 | 0.51 | **4.74** |

At the same predictor budget as the FP8-of-target anchor, the early-exit
predictor codes the 32B cache 1.2 b/s tighter. And unlike the FP8
predictor, the curve extends smoothly down to a few percent of a prefill.
Every point also dominates the natural hybrid competitor (run the FP8
predictor partway and code its layers only): per unit of predictor
compute, a bit-exact bf16 layer eliminates 10.3 bits of cache, while an
FP8-predicted layer eliminates 8.1.

## Why per-token probes cap out, and what that points at

The linear probes contribute much less than the exact prefix does:
predicted-layers-only rates sit between 9.4 and 10.2 b/s against a
no-predictor anchor of 10.33, and the per-layer profile decays from
about 9.0 b/s one block past the exit toward the anchor within four to
eight blocks.

It is tempting to read that as "deep KV is unpredictable", and it would
be wrong. There is a structural reason a per-token map from $h_k$ has to
cap out, however much capacity you give it: the missing computation is
not a per-token function. Layer $l$'s KV at position $t$ depends,
through $l - k$ rounds of attention, on the hidden states of *other*
positions. A probe that sees only $h_k(t)$ cannot represent that mixing,
in the same way that no amount of training lets a pointwise function
compute a convolution. The FP8-of-target predictor codes at 6.26
precisely because it runs the full attention stack and tracks the
mixing; its only sin is rounding noise.

Read this way, the probe numbers are not a verdict, they are a
decomposition. They measure how much of deep KV is carried by the
position's own mid-stack state (up to about 1.6 b/s one block past the
exit, decaying within a few blocks), and how much travels through
attention (the rest). The
predictor-design question becomes concrete: how *narrow* can the
continuation computation be and still carry the attention-mediated
part? The target spends width 4096 on blocks $k..L$; nothing says the
prediction-relevant content of those blocks needs anything close to
that.

## The FP8-native cache, where this gets practical

Deployed caches are increasingly FP8 already (vLLM, SGLang, TRT-LLM
defaults), so the deployment-relevant question is what early exit does
to an FP8 e4m3 cache with per-tensor scales. Same scheme: the prefix
layers reproduce the FP8 cache bit-exactly (same values, same cast),
and the probes code the FP8 symbols of the deeper layers. Qwen3-8B:

| $k$ | $x$ (FLOPs) | b/elem | vs raw FP8 |
| :--- | ---: | ---: | :--- |
| 0  | 0.04 | 5.89 | 1.36x |
| 2  | 0.10 | 5.38 | 1.49x |
| 6  | 0.20 | 4.68 | 1.71x |
| 12 | 0.36 | 3.64 | 2.20x |
| 18 | 0.52 | 2.59 | 3.09x |
| 24 | 0.68 | 1.61 | 4.98x |

Two things to note. The probes earn much more here than in the bf16
case: predicted-layers-only rates run 5.3 to 6.1 bits against a raw
8.0, because an FP8 element has less entropy for the prediction to
miss. And the cheap end is genuinely cheap: the $k = 0$ point is
embeddings, KV projections, and linear probes, about four percent of
a prefill, and it already takes a third off every FP8 cache it
touches. The published FP8-of-target anchor (2.22 b/elem at $x = 0.5$)
still codes tighter at its budget; the curve here is what the budgets
*below* it buy.

And Qwen3-32B, same profile:

| $k$ | $x$ (FLOPs) | b/elem | vs raw FP8 |
| :--- | ---: | ---: | :--- |
| 0  | 0.02 | 5.98 | 1.34x |
| 4  | 0.08 | 5.47 | 1.46x |
| 8  | 0.14 | 5.08 | 1.58x |
| 11 | 0.19 | 4.74 | 1.69x |
| 16 | 0.27 | 4.23 | 1.89x |
| 21 | 0.34 | 3.74 | 2.14x |
| 32 | 0.51 | 2.74 | 2.92x |

The shape repeats at scale: a third off at two percent of a prefill,
2.1x at a third. The FP8-of-target anchor (2.05 b/elem) keeps its crown
at $x = 0.5$; everything below that budget now belongs to early exit.

## Training the continuation

We can measure the per-token term directly: train per-layer MLP heads
from $h_k$ jointly with a per-cell generalised-Gaussian coder (the probe
residual is heavy-tailed, and a GGD prices the tails honestly), under
the coding-length loss itself. The answer barely moves: 9.83 to 9.79 b/s
at $k = 6$. That is the no-attention term measured, not a limit on
prediction; it says the recoverable information travels through the
mixing, so the predictor has to mix.

The experiment that follows is a **narrow continuation transformer**:
project $h_k$ down to width $d' \ll 4096$, run one narrow causal block
(RoPE attention plus MLP) per missing target layer, and read each
layer's KV out of the narrow stream through a learned projection. It is
the missing computation, miniaturised. FLOPs scale as $(d'/4096)^2$ per
block, so a $d' = 1024$ continuation of 29 blocks costs about five
percent of a prefill on top of the $k$-layer prefix. Trained under the
same coding-length loss, on 8x the calibration data (the head runs were
also data-starved: 600M parameters against 131k tokens).

First round of results, twelve thousand steps, random init, on a grid
of $k \in \{6, 12\}$ and $d' \in \{512, 1024\}$, plus a variant whose
narrow stream also emits a per-token $\sigma$ scale (residual size is
content-dependent, and a static per-cell $\sigma$ pays both ways):

| config | pred-layers b/s | vs per-token bound |
| :--- | ---: | :--- |
| $k{=}6$, $d'{=}512$ | 9.72 | bound 9.79 |
| $k{=}6$, $d'{=}1024$ | 9.68 | |
| $k{=}6$, $d'{=}1024$, $\sigma$-head | 9.65 | |
| $k{=}12$, $d'{=}512$ | 9.61 | bound 9.71 |
| $k{=}12$, $d'{=}1024$ | 9.57 | |

The continuation does beat every per-token bound, so attention in the
predictor recovers information no pointwise head can. But honestly
accounted, the first round only moves the frontier a little: at
$k = 12$ it takes the point from $(0.36, 6.28)$ to roughly
$(0.39, 6.12)$. Put per-FLOP: an exact target layer removes about 10.3
bits of whole-cache rate per unit of prefill spent, and this
continuation removes about 3. Until a learned continuation clears the
10.3 bar, the bf16 frontier is simply "how much of the real model you
run", plus small change. The live levers, runs in flight: width (does
$d' = 2048$ bend it), denser supervision (distilling the narrow stream
against projections of the true hidden states), and longer training —
twelve thousand steps on a billion tokens of signal is an early stop,
not an endpoint.

The FP8 regime tells a different story at the cheap end, and it is
worth noticing: there the $k = 0$ probes alone remove 2.1 b/elem for
four percent of a prefill, roughly five times more bit-per-FLOP
efficient than running real layers. Prediction is weak where the
symbols are high-entropy (bf16 tails) and strong where quantisation has
already thrown the tails away. Cheap predictors and coarse caches are
natural partners.

## Where the frontier stands

A position report, not a verdict; every number in this post is a first
pass with obvious headroom in data, capacity, architecture, and coder.

What is already measured. At moderate budgets the early-exit family
wins outright: at the FP8 anchor's own cost it codes the 32B cache 1.2
b/s tighter (4.74 against 5.98), and it reaches the anchor's rate at
72 percent of the anchor's cost on 8B. On FP8-native caches, the kind
deployments actually hold, the four-percent-of-a-prefill point already
buys 1.36x, rising to 2.2x at a third of a prefill. Those are
deployable-shaped numbers: a prefix-cache tier or a cross-datacenter
transfer can pick its point on the curve today.

What is still open is how far down the cheap end goes. Below
$x \approx 0.15$ the bf16 curve currently rides on the exact prefix
alone, and the decomposition above says the rest of the information
travels through attention. The continuation transformer is the live
attack on that region; its results will say whether the
attention-mediated share can be carried at narrow width, or whether it
needs a budget closer to the prefix itself.
